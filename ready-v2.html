Your Performance Readiness Checklist

Close doesn't mean ready. Tick all 6 before you go full send.

Your clearance letter doesn't cover any of this. It says your tissue healed enough to tolerate activity. That's it. These 6 standards are what actually decide whether you're ready to perform — not just survive a game.

The 6 Standards That Actually Matter
Strength Symmetry BBG
  • Your injured leg needs to produce force within 10% of your other leg
  • Below that threshold and your nervous system is compensating on every rep — even if you can't feel it
  • This shows up as a load shift. Your COM drifts to your stronger side on every landing, cut, and drive
  • You can't feel it. Your body just routes around the problem
NCP Target: LSI >90% single-leg strength | BBG L3+ for RTP
Power and Elastic Symmetry
  • Strength and power are different qualities — you can pass strength tests and still be 30% down on power
  • Single-leg hop tests expose this. Most athletes skip them entirely
  • Your elastic system — the stretch-shortening cycle — shuts down after injury and doesn't come back on its own
  • RSI measures how well your tendons store and return energy. Low RSI means you look flat, a step slow, and gas out faster
NCP Target: SL hop LSI >90% | RSI Mod >0.9 | CMJ LSI >90%
Load Tolerance
  • Can you train hard and not pay for it the next day?
  • Swelling, aching, or stiffness 24-48hrs post-session is your tissue still talking
  • That conversation needs to end before you're actually ready
  • If you're managing load instead of loading freely — you're not there yet
NCP Target: Zero reactive swelling or ache >24hrs post-activity
Movement Symmetry — Biomechanics
  • Your body adapts to injury by finding workarounds — compression patterns, load avoidance, rotation bias
  • After injury, athletes often get stuck in an anterior pelvic orientation or lateral shift — loading one hip more than the other without realising
  • This changes how your hip rotates on your drive foot, how you absorb force on landing, and how you generate power off the cut
  • If your hip can't internally rotate freely on your injured side, you will compensate through your knee — every single rep
NCP Target: Hip IR symmetry within 10° bilateral | No lateral COM shift on SL landing
Psychological Readiness
  • This isn't a mindset issue — it's a clinical variable
  • Athletes who return without addressing fear of re-injury are significantly more likely to get hurt again and underperform for months
  • Research shows confidence in cutting and jumping is one of the strongest predictors of actual re-injury — not just performance
  • Your brain needs to catch up with your body. Graded exposure to threatening movements under progressive load is the only way through this
NCP Target: ACL-RSI >65 | Perceived confidence on cuts/jumps >8/10
Sport-Specific Exposure
  • You can't skip the bridge between rehab and full game speed
  • Contested jumps, hard drives, defensive slides, contact — these need progressive reloading over time
  • Not thrown at you on game day and hoped for the best
  • Return to practice ≠ return to performance. Volume, intensity, and contact need to be graded
NCP Target: Progressive RTS exposure over minimum 4 weeks before full competition

Most athletes return when they pass 1 or 2 of these. Passing all 6 is rare — and it's exactly why re-injury rates stay high. This isn't over-cautiousness. It's the difference between returning and returning to your level.
